1

The Morning Ritual Daily Executive Briefing & Inbox Triage — start every day with clarity

6 min
▾
💡 Why this is the anchor prompt

If you only ever use one Cowork prompt, this is the one. It's the difference between starting Monday inside your inbox and starting Monday above it. Run it before you open email — the briefing already knows what's there.

Prompt 1 — Daily Executive Briefing & Inbox Triage

Best forStarting each day with clarity on priorities and urgent emails
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word
Output1-page briefing + 3 draft replies
CadenceEvery morning, 7:30 AM
Review my emails, calendar, and recent documents from the past 24 hours. Create a concise executive briefing (1 page max) with: 1) Top priorities for today (meetings + key topics) 2) Emails that require my response (ranked by urgency) 3) Decisions required today 4) Risks or escalations to be aware of 5) Suggested follow-ups based on commitments I've made Then draft 3 short reply suggestions for my highest-priority emails. Tone: direct, executive-ready, no fluff.
✅ Pro tip — make it Edgewell-shaped

Add one line to the end: "Group emails by brand — Schick, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, premium grooming — and flag any retailer escalation separately." The brief gets sharper instantly.

🎯 Expected outcome

A 1-page brief on the executive's actual day, with three draft replies queued for review. Total time from prompt to reading: under 90 seconds.

2

The Friday Rhythm Weekly Leadership Update + Shareable HTML Dashboard

7 min
▾
💡 Why this matters

The Friday leadership update is the single most common request from any executive's chief of staff. Cowork builds it from a week of your own activity — no manual scraping, no chasing your own team for status. The HTML dashboard is the version you can actually share or save.

Prompt 2 — Weekly Leadership Update Builder

Best forTurning a week of activity into a clear leadership narrative
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word + PowerPoint
Output1-page Word update + 5-slide PPT outline
CadenceEvery Friday, 3:00 PM
Review my Microsoft 365 activity from the past 5 business days (emails, meetings, and documents). Include Teams context only if available. Create: 1) A 1-page Word leadership update with: Key accomplishments, Risks & blockers, Decisions made, Open items, Recommended next steps. 2) A 5-slide PowerPoint outline: Status > Accomplishments > Risks > Decisions needed > Next steps. Write for a senior executive audience. Keep it concise and structured.

Prompt 3 — Weekly Executive Summary Dashboard (Safe HTML)

Best forA clean, browser-ready weekly report — no formatting dependencies
AppsOutlook + Calendar — HTML file output
OutputSelf-contained HTML report you can open or share
Review my emails, calendar, and recent activity over the past 7 days. Summarize: key priorities, major meetings/outcomes (if identifiable), top collaborators, risks/follow-ups. Create a self-contained HTML report with: 1) Title: "Weekly Executive Summary" + date range 2) Key priorities section (bullets) 3) Activity table: Activity Type | Topic | Participants | Summary 4) Top 5 focus areas 5) Risks & follow-ups Design requirements: - Simple HTML with inline CSS only - Clean, modern formatting (black/white + one accent color) - No external libraries, scripts, fonts, or dependencies - Fully self-contained and easy to open in any browser If data is limited, infer where reasonable and clearly label assumptions.
✅ Pro tip — keep the window tight

Cowork is consistently sharper over 5 business days than over "the past month." When output feels thin, the fix is almost always to narrow the window and re-run with explicit names (your top 2 brands, your top 2 retailers).

🎯 Expected outcome

Two artifacts ready to use: a Word memo + slide outline for the Monday leadership team, and an HTML dashboard you can save, screenshot, or send.

3

Meetings to Momentum Meeting Preparation & Follow-Through — in one motion

6 min
▾
💡 Why this matters

The cost of a meeting isn't the hour. It's everything that doesn't happen afterwards because nobody wrote down what was decided. Cowork closes that gap by prepping you going in and drafting the follow-up going out — same prompt, same motion.

Prompt 4 — Meeting Preparation & Follow-Through

Best forWalking in prepared, walking out with the follow-up already drafted
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word
OutputPrep brief + talking points + follow-up email template
Prepare me for my upcoming meeting with [person/team, e.g., the Banana Boat brand team] on [date]. Summarize recent interactions (email + meetings), current topics, open threads, and unresolved questions. Provide: 1) 3 recommended talking points 2) 3 questions I should ask 3) Any risks/sensitivities to watch for Then draft a short follow-up email template I can send after the meeting. Keep it concise and practical.
✅ Pro tip — chain it

After the meeting, hand Cowork the transcript or your notes and ask it to finish the follow-up email it drafted before the meeting. The pre-meeting prep and the post-meeting recap become one continuous loop instead of two separate efforts.

🎤 Facilitator note

Pick the executive's next real meeting on their calendar and run this against it live. Watching Cowork pull the relevant prior emails, name the open threads, and draft the follow-up before the meeting has even started is one of the highest-rated demo moments in this session.

🎯 Expected outcome

A prep brief for a real upcoming meeting, plus a follow-up email template that's already 80% written before the meeting starts.

4

Market Edge Competitor Move Tracker + Retail Partner Health Check

6 min
▾
💡 Why this matters

Edgewell leaders who walk into reviews with the outside read tied to the inside read win the room. Cowork is the only thing in your stack that fuses the public web (what competitors and retailers are doing) with your inbox (what your team has been saying about it).

Prompt 5 — Competitor Move Tracker

Best forStaying ahead of competitor product, pricing, marketing, and leadership moves
AppsWeb + Outlook + Excel + Word
OutputCompetitor scorecard (Excel) + 1-page "so-what" memo
Search the web for the most material moves over the past 30 days from these competitors: [Procter & Gamble / Gillette, Unilever / Dove Men+Care, Beiersdorf / Nivea, Coty, Kenvue, Harry's, Dollar Shave Club, Sun Bum, Coppertone — edit list as needed]. Cover: new products, price changes, marketing campaigns, athlete or celebrity signings, exec hires/departures, earnings or guidance updates, and retail/DTC expansion. Then scan my Outlook (past 30 days) for any internal discussion that references the same competitors and pull the key threads. Create: 1) An Excel scorecard with columns: Competitor | Move | Date | Category | Source | Threat (H/M/L) | Internal mention? | Recommended response 2) A 1-page Word memo titled "Competitor Pulse — [date]" covering: - Top 3 moves that matter to us and why - Where we should defend - Where we should consider going on offense Cite every external claim with a source link. Distinguish confirmed facts from inferred implications.

Prompt 6 — Retail Partner Health Check

Best forPre-call prep on a major retailer with current public + internal context
AppsWeb + Outlook + Excel + Word
OutputPartner brief + talking points + risks to watch
I have an upcoming meeting with [Walmart / Target / CVS / Walgreens / Amazon / Kroger / Costco] on [date]. Pull recent public signals from the web (past 60 days): earnings, store openings/closings, leadership changes, strategy announcements, analyst commentary, and any major news affecting them. Then scan my Outlook (past 90 days) for everything I have on this partner: open threads, commitments made, escalations, sell-through numbers shared, and unresolved questions. Create: 1) A 1-page Word brief titled "[Partner] — Pre-Call Brief" with: - Where they are headed (public read) - Where we stand with them (internal read) - 3 talking points - 3 questions to ask them - 2 risks / sensitivities 2) An Excel tracker with columns: Topic | Last touch | Owner | Status | Next step Keep the brief skim-ready in 2 minutes. Cite every public claim.
🎯 Expected outcome

A competitor pulse memo grounded in the past 30 days, and a retailer pre-call brief that fuses public news with the internal history of the account. Both ready inside ten minutes.

5

Leading Through Change Workforce Transition Pack + Rapid Response Statement Builder

5 min
▾
💡 Why this matters

Personal-care leaders run businesses through real change — portfolio resets, plant transitions, recall windows, retailer escalations. Under pressure, executives need a chief of staff who can turn around a workforce communication or a holding statement in minutes, not hours. These are the two prompts that earn their keep on the worst days.

Prompt 7 — Workforce Transition Communication Pack

Best forTown halls, manager talking points, and sensitive 1:1s during change
AppsOutlook + Word + Teams
OutputTown hall remarks + manager FAQ + retention 1:1 guide
Using the most recent internal communications I have access to (leadership emails, all-hands recaps, HR guidance, and relevant Teams threads), help me prepare a workforce transition communication pack for [team / org / brand unit]. Produce: 1) Town hall remarks (~3 minutes spoken): acknowledge the change, name what stays true, name what is changing, and what people can expect next 2) Manager FAQ: the 10 questions managers are most likely to face, with suggested answer language and what NOT to say 3) Retention 1:1 guide: a short framework for talking with key people who were not impacted but may be unsettled — what to ask, what to commit, what to escalate Tone: empathetic, direct, on-message. No spin, no over-promising. Flag any language that should be reviewed by HR, Legal, or Communications before use.

Prompt 8 — Rapid Response / Crisis Statement Builder

Best forDrafting stakeholder-facing statements under pressure
AppsWord + Outlook + Web
OutputHolding statement + Q&A bank + escalation paths
Situation: [describe the issue in 2–3 lines — what happened, what is public, what is being said]. Build a fast-response statement pack with: 1) Holding statement (under 60 words): for press, social, and inbound inquiries within the first 2 hours 2) Employee message: what people inside the company need to hear and from whom 3) Partner/retailer message: tailored language for key external partners 4) Investor-sensitive language check: phrases to avoid until IR signs off 5) Q&A bank: the 8 toughest questions and prepared answers 6) Escalation paths: which functions must approve before anything goes out (Legal, IR, Comms, HR), and who owns the next update window Tone: calm, factual, accountable. Do not speculate. Flag every assumption.
⚠ Hard rule on these two

Anything externally facing — workforce transition language, holding statements, partner messages — runs through Legal, IR, and Communications before it goes out. Cowork drafts in minutes; the approval chain stays the same. Treat these outputs as sharp starting points, never final copy.

🎯 Expected outcome

Two playbooks in your back pocket for the days you hope never come — and the confidence that when they do, you've got a first draft inside five minutes instead of five hours.

The Board Narrative — Capstone Quarterly Executive Narrative Builder — the deliverable that justifies the session

5 min
▾
💡 Why this is the close

The first five labs are the rhythm. This one is the proof. Watching a 90-day quarter become a 2-page memo + 8-slide deck + metrics table in a single prompt is the moment executives stop asking "what is Cowork" and start asking "when can I have it."

Prompt 9 — Quarterly Executive Narrative Builder

Best forTurning a quarter of activity + market context into a polished executive story
AppsWeb + Outlook + Word + PowerPoint + Excel
Output2-page narrative + 8-slide deck + metrics table
CadenceQuarterly, ahead of board prep
Build the story of the past quarter for an executive audience. Step 1 — Internal: review my Outlook (past 90 days) and any attached Excel / Word files I reference, and surface: wins, key decisions, open risks, top stakeholder feedback, and commitments made across our brand portfolio (Schick, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, premium grooming). Step 2 — External: pull web context on the personal-care category over the same quarter (competitor moves, consumer trends, channel shifts, macro signals) so the story is told against the market — not in a vacuum. Create: 1) A 2-page Word memo titled "Q[X] Narrative — [my portfolio]" with: - Opening: the market this quarter (3 lines) - What we did and why it mattered - Wins, risks, and what we are watching - Recommended focus for next quarter 2) An 8-slide PowerPoint executive deck: 1: Market context 2: Our quarter at a glance 3–5: Top 3 wins (one slide each) 6: Risks & mitigations 7: Stakeholder voice (quoted feedback themes) 8: Where we go next 3) A simple Excel table tracking the metrics I referenced in emails so the deck has hard numbers behind every claim. Tone: confident, factual, story-driven. Cite external claims. Label any inference clearly so I can validate before sharing.
🎤 Facilitator note — close on this

Don't ship the deck during the session. Skim it together. The point isn't the artifact — it's the executive realizing this is what they could be doing every quarter before every board cycle. End with: "Imagine running this every Friday, every quarter, before every board meeting. That's the rhythm."

🎯 Expected outcome

A 2-page narrative memo, an 8-slide executive deck, and a metrics table — all grounded in the executive's own portfolio. The leave-behind that proves the 45 minutes was worth it.

Five rules that make Cowork sharper

  • Keep time windows tight — 24 hours or 5 business days, not "the past month."
  • Name names — your top 2–3 brands, retailers, or stakeholders. Vague in, vague out.
  • Cap the length — "1 page max" or "assume I'll skim in 2 minutes" changes the output dramatically.
  • Re-run with sharper context — if the first pass feels thin, the second pass with names + tighter window almost always lands.
  • Save what works — the prompts that produce output you'd want again belong on a cadence.

🛠 Troubleshooting

IssueLikely fix
Cowork output feels generic Narrow the time window, name 3–8 specific priorities or stakeholders, and add "1 page max". Re-run.
The briefing missed a key thread Cowork searches by relevance, not exhaustively. Name the brand, retailer, or person explicitly in your next run.
The weekly update reads like a list, not a story Add to the prompt: "Write this as a narrative for a senior executive audience, not bullet points."
Cowork drafted an email — but I didn't want it to send It hasn't. Cowork drafts; you approve. Nothing leaves your tenant without an explicit confirmation from you.
The HTML dashboard looks broken Save the file and open in a browser. Some email previews strip inline CSS — the file itself renders correctly.
The competitor tracker missed obvious news Web search is bounded by what's published and indexed. Add the date range explicitly and re-run with named competitors.
The quarterly narrative inferred something incorrect Cowork labels inferences. Spot them, correct them in your version, and the next run will hold the correction.
I want to set a recurring run but don't know how Ask Cowork directly: "Set this prompt to run every weekday at 7:30 AM." Confirm when prompted.
