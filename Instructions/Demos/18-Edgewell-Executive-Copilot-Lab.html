Overview — why this lab exists

Personal care is being reshaped at the same time leaders are being asked to move faster: shopper behavior is fragmenting across mass, club, e-comm and Amazon; private-label is pressuring margin in shave and sun care; Gen-Z has changed what "brand love" means for Schick, Banana Boat, Billie, Bulldog and Jack Black; and the supply chain is no longer a back-office function — it's a board topic.

This immersion is not a feature tour of Copilot. It's a leadership simulation. Over four connected scenarios, you'll move through a realistic week for an Edgewell executive — coming back from a plant tour, getting your operating rhythm back, sharpening a brand & competitive view, and walking out with a board-ready decision pack.

How to use this guide

Self-paced executive

Move top to bottom. Each scenario opens with "The story" — read that first. Open one lab at a time, copy the prompt, run it in your tenant, then come back and check your work against the Expected outcome and Self-check.

Facilitator-led immersion

Each lab has a Facilitator note with talking points and timing. Recommended cadence: 5–7 minutes per lab, with a 10-minute debrief at the end of each scenario. Capstone D3 should be run as a live group activity.

Verify before you decide Every output in this lab is a draft, not a decision. When a number, a quote, or a customer name shows up in a Copilot answer, click through to the source. Treat agents the way you'd treat a sharp analyst on their first day at Edgewell — talented, fast, and not yet accountable for the brand.

A1

Voice catch-up on mobile — your first 10 minutes off the plane

Time: 6 min Tool: Copilot mobile app, voice mode Goal: Get a verbal briefing while you walk to the car Level: Basic
▾

You don't read on the curb. You listen. Open the M365 Copilot mobile app, tap the voice icon, and have a conversation with Copilot the way you would with your chief of staff. The whole point of this lab is to break the habit of "opening email first." You're a leader, not an inbox.

Try this — say it out loud
I just landed after two weeks visiting our Sayreville and Milford plants, plus the Walmart and Target buyer teams. Give me a verbal briefing in two minutes: what's the most urgent thing waiting for me, what decisions are blocked on me, and what one piece of good news I should know before Monday. Keep it conversational, not a list.
Follow-up — drill in
Pause on the Banana Boat point you just made. Who exactly is asking for the decision, what's the deadline, and is anything on hold because I haven't responded? Read me the most recent message thread.
Closing voice prompt — set the trajectory of your week
Based on everything that came in while I was traveling, what are the three themes I should walk into Monday's executive staff meeting prepared to discuss? Give me a one-sentence point of view for each.
Expected outcome A two-minute verbal summary that names: (1) the most time-sensitive item waiting for your decision (likely a customer or buyer-team escalation), (2) something blocked on you (likely a sign-off, a comp plan, or a launch gate), and (3) a positive signal (a brand win, a chargeback resolved, a buyer note). You should be able to repeat all three out loud without notes.
Tip — make it sound like you In Copilot mobile settings, choose the voice that doesn't feel like a robot. Set the tone to "professional, concise." A good Copilot voice briefing sounds like a smart EA giving you the read on the room — not like a podcast.
Don't act on voice alone Voice is for situational awareness, not for decisions. If Copilot tells you "the Costco renegotiation is at risk," you go back to the source thread before you call the buyer team. Voice is the wrapper, the email is the truth.
Self-check
  • I can name the single most urgent decision waiting for me.
  • I know who I owe a reply to before Monday morning.
  • I did this entirely with voice — I never opened my inbox.
Facilitator note Open this lab with a question to the room: "When you got back from your last business trip, how many hours did it take you to feel caught up?" Most exec answers are "4 to 8 hours." This lab is positioned against that number. Have one volunteer do this with voice on speaker so the room hears Copilot speaking back.
A2

Mobile triage — Outlook threads + Teams chats, summarized

Time: 8 min Tool: Outlook mobile, Teams mobile (Copilot inside both) Goal: Don't read 487 emails — let Copilot read them for you Level: Basic
▾

You're going to use Copilot inside the mobile Office apps. Same prompts as desktop — the difference is you can do it from the back of a car. Open Outlook mobile, find a long thread (a Walmart line review thread, a Banana Boat seasonality discussion, a supply chain escalation), tap into it, and use the Copilot icon.

In a long Outlook thread on mobile
Summarize this thread in five bullets. Then tell me: what is the actual ask of me, what's the deadline, and is there a position the team has already converged on that I can either endorse or push back on? Use Edgewell-specific language — brand names, retailer names, channel names.
In a Teams chat catch-up on mobile
I've been off Teams for two weeks. Summarize my unread messages across all my chats. Group them into: (1) needs my decision, (2) FYI from my direct reports, (3) congratulations or recognition I should respond to, (4) noise I can ignore. For each in groups 1 and 3, draft a one-line reply I can send.
Expected outcome You should leave this lab with two artifacts: (1) a 5-bullet summary of the most important email thread that arrived while you were away, and (2) a sorted Teams catch-up with at least 3 ready-to-send replies you only need to tap "send" on.
Tip — let it write the reply In Outlook mobile, after the thread summary, ask: "Draft a reply that endorses the team's position and asks for X." Then send. You just cleared a five-day-old decision in 90 seconds.
Real-world note Edgewell executives report this is the single highest-leverage Copilot habit they've adopted. The math is simple: 30 long threads × 4 minutes each = two hours. Copilot summarization × those same 30 threads ≈ 15 minutes.
Self-check
  • I never opened a thread without first asking Copilot to summarize it.
  • I sent at least one reply from a Copilot-generated draft.
  • I left at least one thread on "FYI — ignore" without guilt.
A3

Inbox & calendar — Copilot in Outlook on the desktop

Time: 10 min Tool: Copilot in Outlook (desktop or web) Goal: Inbox down to a working set; calendar reflecting your priorities Level: Basic / Intermediate
▾

You're at your desk now. The mobile catch-up gave you situational awareness. Outlook is where you actually clear the decks. First, give Copilot in Outlook a sense of who you are using custom instructions. Then use it to prioritize, draft, and schedule.

Step 1 — set Copilot in Outlook's custom instructions

Open Outlook → Copilot pane → Custom instructions. Paste:

Custom instructions for an Edgewell executive
I am a senior executive at Edgewell Personal Care. Our portfolio includes Schick, Edge, Skintimate, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, Playtex, Stayfree, Carefree, Bulldog, Jack Black, Cremo, and Billie. Our top channels are Walmart, Target, Costco, Amazon, CVS, Walgreens, Kroger, and international. Always: (1) draft email replies in a warm but executive tone — direct, no filler, no exclamation points; (2) when summarizing, lead with the decision needed, not the chronology; (3) if you reference data, name the source thread or document; (4) prefer brevity — three sentences over five — unless I ask for depth; (5) if a reply would commit Edgewell to a number, a date, or a public statement, flag it for me before sending.

Step 2 — prioritize the inbox

In the Copilot pane in Outlook
Look at my inbox from the last 14 days. Group the unread items into: (1) needs a decision from me this week, (2) Walmart / Target / Amazon buyer team escalations, (3) brand-team launches with a gate I need to approve, (4) HR or people-leadership items, (5) anything from my direct reports' direct reports — I want to catch flags that didn't bubble up. For each item in groups 1–4 give me the sender, the ask in one sentence, and a recommended action.

Step 3 — draft three replies in your voice

Use on the top three items from step 2
Draft a reply to this thread. Endorse the team's recommendation, but ask them to come back with: (a) the expected impact on Banana Boat margin for the sun care season, (b) the contingency if the supplier slips by two weeks, and (c) who owns the Walmart conversation if we go that direction. Three sentences, no filler, sign off with my first name.

Step 4 — let Copilot find time on your calendar

Schedule the decisions you can't make over email
From the items I just triaged, identify the three that should become 25-minute decision meetings this week instead of email back-and-forth. For each, propose: (1) the right attendees from my org (use the most likely names from the threads), (2) two candidate times that work given my existing calendar, (3) a meeting title that names the decision, not the topic, and (4) a one-paragraph agenda I can paste into the invite.
Expected outcome Inbox triaged into five named buckets; three replies drafted in your voice and ready to send; three meetings scheduled with decision-named titles. You should be able to close Outlook and feel that the day is yours, not your inbox's.
Verify before sending Read every Copilot-drafted reply once. Look for: invented commitments ("we will ship by June 1"), invented numbers, invented attendees. Copilot is excellent at sounding like you. It is sometimes wrong about facts. You sign the email.
Self-check
  • My custom instructions describe me and Edgewell, not just "be helpful."
  • I sent at least one Copilot-drafted reply with edits.
  • I converted at least one email thread into a calendar invite with a decision-named title.
Facilitator note The custom-instructions step is the moment most executives lean forward. Anchor it: "This is the difference between a generic assistant and one that knows Edgewell." Pull up two exec volunteers' instructions and compare — the differences are illuminating.
A4

Teams meeting recap & live facilitator — walk in already caught up

Time: 10 min Tool: Teams (full client) with Copilot in meetings Goal: Catch up on a meeting you missed; experience live facilitator Level: Basic / Intermediate
▾

You were out for two weeks. The executive staff meeting happened twice without you. The Banana Boat S&OP review happened. The Schick growth review happened. You don't watch 60-minute recordings. You ask Copilot to be in the room for you, retroactively.

Part 1 — Catch up on a recorded meeting

Open Teams → Chat → find the recorded meeting → click "Recap" → open the Copilot tab.

In the meeting recap Copilot pane
I missed this meeting. In 60 seconds of reading, I need to know: (1) what was decided, (2) what was deferred and why, (3) any commitments my team made on my behalf, (4) what's the open question that the room couldn't answer. Then tell me — given who was in the room and what was said — what should I follow up on first?
Drill in on a name or topic
What did [Brand President name] say specifically about the Banana Boat tariff exposure? Quote directly if possible, then summarize their underlying position. Did anyone in the room push back?

Part 2 — Live facilitator in a meeting that's about to start

Join your next Teams meeting (it can be a small internal review). Turn Copilot on. During the meeting, prompt Copilot live:

Mid-meeting, as facilitator
We've been in this meeting for 15 minutes. What are the points of agreement so far, what are we still circling on, and is there a decision that's been implicitly made that no one has stated out loud? Don't summarize the chronology — summarize the state of the room right now.
Closing the meeting
In the last five minutes of this meeting, generate: (1) the decisions made, (2) the owner and due date for each next step, (3) the explicit open questions we're carrying forward. Format as something I can paste into a Teams chat to the attendees.
Expected outcome You can walk into Monday already current on two meetings you missed. You ran a meeting that ended with decisions + owners + dates posted to chat — not a transcript no one will read.
Quote attribution Copilot transcription is good, not perfect. Before you cite a teammate's quote to a third party ("Brand President X said Banana Boat will hit 12% growth"), verify in the transcript. Especially for accents, talking-over moments, and numbers.
Self-check
  • I asked Copilot what someone said, not just what was discussed.
  • I posted a decisions-and-owners block to the meeting chat at the end.
  • I caught one implicit decision that hadn't been stated explicitly.
Facilitator note Pre-record a 10-minute scripted meeting before the workshop (use Copilot to script it — see the source document's "Record a Meeting" approach). Play it back in the room with two devices, then have execs ask the recap pane the prompts above. This makes the lab demo-able even for executives who don't have a relevant real meeting yet.
Scenario A wrap — what you should now have A two-minute voice briefing. A triaged inbox. Three drafts sent. Three decision meetings scheduled. Two missed meetings caught up on. One live meeting that ended with crisp decisions. This is the operating posture we'll build on for the rest of the immersion.

B1

Copilot Chat + Prompt Coach — the executive's prompting style

Time: 8 min Tool: Copilot Chat (m365.cloud.microsoft/chat) with Prompt Coach Goal: Learn the GCSE pattern (Goal · Context · Source · Expectations) Level: Intermediate
▾

Most executives prompt the way they ask their EA on a busy Tuesday — too short. Copilot can do extraordinary things when you give it four pieces: Goal (what success looks like), Context (your role and the Edgewell situation), Source (where to ground), Expectations (length, tone, format).

A weak prompt — try this first
Write me something about Banana Boat for the leadership meeting.

Now turn on Prompt Coach (the lightbulb icon in Copilot Chat) and resubmit. Prompt Coach will rewrite your prompt. Notice what it adds.

A strong executive prompt — GCSE pattern
GOAL: Prepare a 1-page pre-read for Thursday's executive staff meeting on Banana Boat's sun care season. CONTEXT: I'm the Brand President for sun care. We've had two consecutive seasons of share loss to Coppertone in mass and Sun Bum in club. Walmart is signaling a planogram redesign for spring. SOURCES: Pull from my OneDrive sun-care marketing folder, the most recent Banana Boat S&OP recap in Teams, and our latest Nielsen scan data if it's in SharePoint. EXPECTATIONS: 1 page, executive tone, no exclamation points. Lead with the decision the room needs to make Thursday — not the chronology. Three sections: (1) what the data is saying, (2) two strategic options with risks, (3) my recommended position with the open question I want the team to challenge. End with a single 'ask of the room.'
Ask Prompt Coach to teach you
Compare my first prompt to my second. As Prompt Coach, tell me — in plain English — exactly what made the second prompt produce a better answer. Then give me a reusable template for executive pre-read prompts I can apply to any brand at Edgewell.
Expected outcome Two side-by-side answers that make the difference obvious. A reusable Edgewell pre-read template — you should be able to swap "Banana Boat" for "Schick" or "Billie" and the prompt still works.
Tip — name the decision The single most powerful word in an executive prompt is decision. "Help me decide between X and Y" outperforms "Tell me about X and Y" almost every time.
Self-check
  • I can explain GCSE without notes.
  • I saved at least one reusable prompt template.
  • I noticed that "lead with the decision" changes the structure of the answer.
B2

Personalization & Memory — teach Copilot who you are at Edgewell

Time: 8 min Tool: Copilot Chat → Personalization → Memory Goal: A Copilot that doesn't make you re-explain your role every time Level: Intermediate
▾

In Copilot Chat, open Personalization. Set or refresh your memory. The goal is that next time you type "build the brand review," Copilot already knows which brand, which channels, and the tone of the executive who's asking.

Seed your memory — paste once
Please remember the following about me, and apply it to every future answer unless I say otherwise. ROLE: I'm a senior leader at Edgewell Personal Care (NYSE: EPC). My remit covers [insert your portfolio — e.g. shave & feminine care / sun & skin / wet shave / all brands]. KEY BRANDS: Schick, Edge, Skintimate, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, Playtex, Stayfree, Carefree, Bulldog, Jack Black, Cremo, Billie. KEY CHANNELS: Walmart, Target, Costco, Amazon, CVS, Walgreens, Kroger, club, international. PEER SET I CARE ABOUT: Energizer Holdings, Church & Dwight, Kenvue, Procter & Gamble, Unilever, Edgewell's direct competitors in razors (Gillette / Harry's), sun (Coppertone / Sun Bum / Neutrogena), and feminine (Tampax / Always / Rael). HOW I LIKE TO RECEIVE INFORMATION: lead with the decision; concise executive tone; no exclamation points; cite sources; flag when you're guessing.
Test the memory
Without me re-stating my role: build me a one-paragraph point of view on what Edgewell's biggest near-term threat is in the wet-shave category, and the single strategic move I should be considering.
Expected outcome The second prompt is one short sentence — but the answer reflects your role, brands, and peer set without you having to repeat them. That's the leverage of memory.
Memory is editable Anything you've put in memory is visible in Settings → Personalization. Remove sensitive items there. If you change roles internally, refresh memory the same day — Copilot will keep behaving like your old role until you tell it otherwise.
Self-check
  • My memory profile names my role, my brands, my channels, and my peer set.
  • I tested a short prompt and got an answer that "sounds like Edgewell."
  • I know how to delete or edit a memory entry.
B3

A day in the life — building your reusable executive prompt library

Time: 10 min Tool: Copilot Chat, your OneDrive notes Goal: Five prompts you'll actually reuse every week Level: Intermediate
▾

The best executive Copilot users don't write new prompts daily — they reuse five great ones. Run each of the following in your tenant, edit the output for yourself, and save the final prompt into a OneNote or a OneDrive file called "My Copilot prompt library."

1 · Monday morning rhythm
Based on my calendar, my unread inbox, and any new files shared with me since Friday, give me a Monday brief: (1) the three most important meetings this week and what I should pre-think for each, (2) anyone on my team who appears blocked or escalating, (3) any external signal (retailer escalation, supplier issue, news mention of an Edgewell brand) I should be aware of. Half a page, executive tone.
2 · Pre-1:1 brief on a direct report
Before my 1:1 with [name], summarize: (1) what they've been working on based on our shared chats and files in the last two weeks, (2) what they've explicitly asked me for or escalated, (3) one piece of recognition I could give them based on something specific they did, (4) one open question I should ask. Half a page max.
3 · Customer / buyer-team prep
I have a meeting with the Walmart buyer team on [date] covering [Schick / Banana Boat / Playtex — pick]. From everything Edgewell-internal in my tenant — emails, files, recent Teams discussions — give me: (1) where we stand on the JBP, (2) any open commitments either side has made, (3) any tension points (out-of-stock, chargebacks, planogram, promotion ROI), (4) the one ask I should walk in with.
4 · End-of-day reflection
Based on my Teams chats, sent items, and meetings today, summarize: (1) what I decided today, (2) what I committed to and need to follow through on, (3) anyone I said I'd get back to and haven't. Tomorrow's top three priorities given what slipped today.
5 · Brand-of-the-week deep dive
This week I want to go deep on [brand]. Look across our internal data and recent files. Give me: (1) the three things going right, (2) the three things going wrong, (3) the one strategic question this brand's leadership team should be wrestling with that they probably aren't yet. Cite the documents you used.
Expected outcome A OneDrive file titled "My Copilot prompt library — [your name]" with at least five prompts you'd actually reuse. Bonus: a calendar reminder on Monday at 7:45 AM to run prompt #1.
Tip — share the library, not the answer The leverage compounds when you share your prompt with your leadership team — not your answer. Their context (brand, channel, region) will yield a different, better-for-them answer from the same prompt.
Self-check
  • My library has at least five prompts and they're saved somewhere I'll find them again.
  • At least one prompt is grounded against my own files, not the open web.
  • I shared one prompt with a peer or a direct report.
B4

Copilot Search — find anything in the Edgewell universe

Time: 6 min Tool: Copilot Search across M365 + connected sources Goal: Stop searching three apps. Ask one place. Level: Basic
▾

Copilot Search is the unified search across your tenant — SharePoint, OneDrive, Teams, Outlook, plus connected sources if your IT team has wired them in (think a CRM, the internal HR site, the Edgewell wiki, internal financial systems). The trick is asking like an analyst, not like Google.

Like Google — don't do this
Banana Boat 2025 marketing plan
Like an analyst — do this instead
Find the most recent version of the Banana Boat sun care marketing plan or growth plan. If there are multiple versions, list them, tell me which one was last edited, by whom, and where it lives. Then summarize the top three strategic priorities it commits to. If there isn't one in our tenant, tell me — don't invent it.
Knowledge-trail prompt
I'm trying to understand Edgewell's position on private-label competition in shave and feminine care. Find the three most authoritative internal documents on that topic, name the authors, and synthesize the through-line. Where do they agree, where do they disagree, and what's the most recent strategic position we've publicly taken?
Expected outcome Copilot returns named documents with named authors. You can follow up on the people, not just the files. You learn the difference between "find me a file" (weak) and "find me the position" (strong).
If Copilot can't find it, it shouldn't fabricate it Watch for confident-sounding answers with no citations. If Copilot Search can't find a document on a topic, it should tell you so. If it doesn't — push back: "What's the file name and last modified date?"
Self-check
  • I found a document I had forgotten existed.
  • I got a named author I can follow up with.
  • I asked Copilot to tell me when it couldn't find something — and it did.
Facilitator note Use this lab to surface the "Copilot quality is a data hygiene problem" conversation. The execs who get the best answers have the most disciplined SharePoint. This is the place to invite a comment from IT or the CIO if they're in the room.
Scenario B wrap You now have: a sharper prompting style (GCSE), a Copilot that knows you, a five-prompt library you'll actually use, and a search habit that replaces three apps. This is your operating rhythm. From here, we go strategic.

Analyst Agent — Edgewell vs. peers, from the 10-Ks

Mid-meeting — read the room

Common executive questions — short answers

"Is what I type confidential?"Yes — within your Edgewell tenant, Copilot inherits Edgewell's data protections. Your prompts and responses are not used to train foundation models. Same data boundary as the rest of M365.
"What if Copilot makes something up?"It can. That's why every claim needs a source, and why this lab insists on the verification habit. The executive's job is judgment — Copilot accelerates the inputs, not the judgment.
"Can my team see my prompts?"No. Your chats are private to you unless you explicitly share a Notebook or Page. Memory is personal to your account.
"What about IP and PII?"Sensitivity labels apply. Don't paste personal data of consumers into prompts. For M&A or board-sensitive work, follow your normal handling rules — Copilot inherits the label, it doesn't override it.
