1

Monday on the Move Copilot Voice + Mobile — catch up after a market tour

▾
💡 Why this matters

The most expensive minutes of your week are the first 90 on Monday morning. Edgewell leaders rarely get a clean desk start — you're walking from a flight, a market visit, or a retailer review back into a packed inbox and a Tuesday board prep. Copilot Voice on mobile turns the car ride or airport walk into a working catch-up — without thumbs on glass.

The scenario

You've spent two weeks visiting plants, walking stores with key retail partners, and meeting with brand teams in market. You land Sunday night. Monday's calendar is already full. You need to know — before you sit down — what slipped, what's burning, and what to handle first.

Prompt 1 — Open with voice

Best forStarting Monday with clarity, hands-free
AppsCopilot mobile · voice
OutputAction list + follow-ups, read aloud
Hey Copilot, summarize the emails, Teams messages, and meetings I missed from [direct report or key person] over the last two weeks, and create a prioritized action list for me to handle today.

Prompt 2 — The full catch-up

Hey Copilot, summarize my emails, Teams messages, and channel messages from the last workday. Group them by brand or initiative — Schick, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, premium grooming, and any retailer escalations — and tell me the top 5 things I need to handle today.
✅ Pro tip

Voice prompts can be conversational. Once you hear the summary, say "Open the one about Banana Boat first" or "Draft a quick reply to the Walmart thread." Treat it like a chief of staff sitting next to you, not a search box.

🎤 Facilitator note

Demo this before showing the desktop experience. Executives consistently rate the voice-on-mobile moment as the highest "wow" of the session. If voice isn't viable in the room, walk through the steps verbally and play a short pre-recorded clip.

🎯 Expected outcome

A spoken (or on-screen) action list of the top items waiting for you — grouped by brand and ranked by urgency — before you've opened a laptop.

2

The Mobile Briefing Outlook + Teams on mobile — summarize threads and meeting recaps on the go

▾
💡 Why this matters

Most executive decisions don't happen at a desk. You'll read the long Banana Boat sun-care promo thread on the way to a meeting; you'll catch the missed Hawaiian Tropic NPD review from the back of a car. The point of Copilot on mobile isn't more reading — it's less, but better.

The scenario

Between the airport and the office, you've got 20 minutes. There's a 47-message Outlook thread on a price-pack change for Schick at a key mass retailer, and a Teams meeting recording for the Banana Boat summer readiness review you missed last Friday. Both matter. Neither deserves your full attention right now — but the decisions buried in them do.

Step 1 — Summarize an Outlook thread on mobile

  1. Open the long thread in Outlook mobile.
  2. Tap the Copilot icon → choose Summary by Copilot.
  3. Then ask Copilot a sharper question against the same thread:
Summarize this thread and tell me: what decision is being asked of me, who is waiting on a response, and what's the recommended next step. Flag any commercial or commitment language I should be careful about.

Step 2 — Recap a Teams meeting you missed

  1. Open the Teams meeting in mobile (transcription/recording must be on).
  2. Tap into the recap and ask Copilot:
Summarize the key decisions, action items, and risks raised in this meeting. Who committed to what, by when? What concerns were raised that I should follow up on personally?
✅ Pro tip

Ask Copilot for the "so-what" not the recap. "What changed because of this meeting?" and "What would my team expect me to follow up on?" produce sharper output than "summarize."

🎯 Expected outcome

Two threads — one email, one meeting — fully understood and triaged in under 10 minutes, with a clear next move on each before you walk into your first meeting.

3

Make Copilot Your Chief of Staff Custom Instructions, Memory, and Prompt Coach — set Copilot up to think like an Edgewell leader

▾
💡 Why this matters

Out of the box, Copilot is a smart generalist. With ten minutes of setup, it becomes your chief of staff — one that knows you run a personal-care portfolio, prioritizes margin and brand equity, weighs retail-partner sensitivity, and writes the way you write. This is the single highest-ROI ten minutes in the entire lab.

Step 1 — Set your Custom Instructions

In m365copilot.com → "…" menu → Settings → Personalization → Custom Instructions. Paste a starter set and adapt it.

Starter: GM, Brand Portfolio (adapt to your role)

My priorities are driving brand growth, protecting margin across our personal-care portfolio, and strengthening our position with key retailers. When providing insights or recommendations, focus on strategic decisions that impact share, brand equity, channel performance, and consumer loyalty. Summarize complex data into clear, actionable points suitable for executive decision-making. Communication should be visionary, concise, and emphasize long-term impact. Prefer internal documents, emails, and chats over external sources when answering questions or looking for information. In meeting summaries, clearly call out decisions made. Use bolding or bullet points to make them stand out. Share clear and truthful feedback in a respectful tone and focus on offering constructive suggestions when something needs improvement.

Starter: CFO

My focus is on revenue protection, cost optimization, and financial sustainability across our personal-care portfolio. When suggesting strategies, emphasize risk management, compliance, and cost reduction opportunities across operations, supply chain, and channel investments. Present financial data in a structured, easy-to-interpret format with clear implications for profitability and shareholder value. Communication should be analytical, concise, and highlight measurable outcomes.

Starter: CIO / Technology Leader

My objectives include leveraging technology to optimize operations, reduce costs in manufacturing and logistics, and enhance digital experiences for our consumers and employees. When making recommendations, prioritize scalable solutions, cybersecurity, and emerging technologies that align with business goals. Provide technical insights in a clear, business-oriented manner, highlighting ROI and operational efficiency. Communication should be precise, forward-looking, and solution-driven.

Step 2 — Generate role-based prompts with Prompt Coach

Use Prompt Coach to generate better prompts — not just write your own. Paste this into Copilot Chat:

I am the [CEO / CFO / CMO / GM] of Edgewell Personal Care. I am a [new / intermediate / advanced] AI user. Please generate 5 complex Copilot prompts that would be relevant to my role across our brand portfolio (Schick, Banana Boat, Hawaiian Tropic, Wet Ones, and our premium grooming brands). Please ensure these prompts use sophisticated prompt engineering techniques.

Step 3 — Improve a prompt you already use

Analyze my prompt and provide feedback on how I could improve it to work better with the Researcher agent: I work in brand management at Edgewell. I am looking into wellness and self-care trends. Generate some ideas of how to extend our product lines into this space.

Improved version (after Prompt Coach):

I work in brand management at Edgewell Personal Care, focusing on our sun-care and premium grooming brands. I am researching wellness and self-care trends for personal-care consumers aged 25–44. Please identify emerging trends, consumer needs, and competitor innovations in this space and suggest 5–10 actionable product extension ideas. Include examples from reputable sources like market research reports, beauty and wellness trade publications, and competitor product lines. Present the findings in a structured format with trend categories, supporting evidence, and product concept suggestions.

Step 4 — Memories (the long game)

Anything you tell Copilot in chat about how you work, your priorities, your team, or your style — you can save as a memory. Over time, this builds a profile that compounds the value of every prompt.

✅ Pro tip

Don't try to write the perfect Custom Instructions in one sitting. Start with one paragraph. After a week, ask Copilot: "Based on how we've been working, what should I add to my Custom Instructions?" Let it propose the refinements.

🎯 Expected outcome

Custom Instructions saved, at least one role-based prompt generated by Prompt Coach, and a memory or two captured. From this point on, every prompt in the lab gets sharper output because Copilot knows who you are.

4

The Day-in-the-Life Catch-Up Day-in-the-life prompts + Microsoft Copilot Search — get current, fast

▾
💡 Why this matters

Every Edgewell leader has the same five questions every morning: What slipped? What's burning? What's next? What do I need to know? Who needs me? These prompts answer all five — fast. Save the ones that work into your Prompt Gallery, and you'll never start a day cold again.

1 · Catching up

Summarize emails and chats I was mentioned in yesterday. Highlight anything that needs my response today.
What should be on my radar from emails last week related to [Schick / Banana Boat / Hawaiian Tropic / retailer name / project]?
Search my recent Teams messages and channels for conversations about [topic] and give me a short brief.

2 · Internal & external meetings

Search my calendar over the next two weeks and list my next meetings with [person or retail partner], including purpose and prep notes.
Help me prepare for my upcoming meeting about [meeting subject]. Summarize recent emails, chats, and documents related to it.
Summarize my recent meeting notes and highlight any decisions made and open items that still need to be resolved.

3 · Task & status checking

Check emails that I still need to reply to and prioritize them into: urgent today, this week, and later.
Summarize [topic]-related chats and emails from [person or team] and list open questions or decisions.

4 · Information gathering

Find the documents, emails, or chats that explain [internal term or initiative]. Give me a simple explanation and key links.
Summarize the key points in this file /[filename] in no more than 10 bullet points.
Show data insights from the last month related to [metric/project] based on available reports and dashboards.

5 · People leadership

Search emails and messages with [direct report] and suggest colleagues who could give feedback on their performance.

6 · Profile your work rhythm

Look across recent documents, emails, chat activity, and file edits to identify times of day when I do my most focused work, creative work, and reactive work. Create a profile of my natural work rhythm and give suggestions for structuring my days that support energy, boundaries, and recovery. Consider optional guidance — such as early creative bursts, mid-morning focus, and late-afternoon wrap-up — but frame this as flexible, not prescriptive, so I can adapt principles to my own energy patterns. Emphasize autonomy and recovery between peaks. Provide me with a list of M365 Copilot features that would support me at different times of my day.

7 · The Super Summarizer

Summarize my emails, Teams meetings, IMs, and channel messages from the last workday. List action items in a dedicated column. Suggest follow-ups if possible, in a dedicated column. The table should look like this: Type (Mail / Teams / Channel) | Topic | Summarization | Action item | Follow-up. If I have been directly mentioned, make the font of the topic bold.

8 · Enterprise catch-up after time away

I was on a market tour for the past two weeks. Please find the [initiative or brand name, e.g., Banana Boat 2026 launch] information and related communications. Summarize what changed, what decisions were made, and what's still open.
✅ Pro tip — save the ones that work

When a prompt produces output you'd want again next week, hover over your prompt → Save Prompt → name it clearly (e.g., "Monday Catch-Up — Brand Portfolio"). Build a personal gallery of 5–10 prompts that are your operating rhythm.

🎯 Expected outcome

You've run at least three of these against your own data, saved two to your Prompt Gallery, and identified the one you'll use every morning starting tomorrow.

5

Cowork: Run Your Leadership Rhythm From "Copilot helps me draft" to "Cowork runs my week"

▾
💡 What changes with Cowork

Copilot helps you think and draft — writing, summarizing, answering questions for individual tasks.
Cowork helps you run your work rhythm. It connects your email, calendar, and documents to give you a single, actionable picture of your day, week, and priorities.

The value of Cowork isn't that it does things Copilot can't do at all — it's that it does them in one motion instead of five. The difference between a capable colleague who can write when asked, and a chief of staff who already knows your week, your priorities, and what needs to happen next.

How to use these prompts: Copy → adjust the brackets → paste into Cowork → review outputs → approve actions only when ready. No emails, files, or calendar invites are sent without your explicit approval.

Prompt 1 — Daily Executive Briefing & Inbox Triage

Best forStarting each day with clarity on priorities and urgent emails
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word
Output1-page briefing + 3 draft replies
Review my emails, calendar, and recent documents from the past 24 hours. Create a concise executive briefing (1 page max) with: 1) Top priorities for today (meetings + key topics) 2) Emails that require my response (ranked by urgency) 3) Decisions required today 4) Risks or escalations to be aware of 5) Suggested follow-ups based on commitments I've made Then draft 3 short reply suggestions for my highest-priority emails. Tone: direct, executive-ready, no fluff.

Prompt 2 — Weekly Leadership Update Builder

Best forTurning a week of activity into a clear leadership narrative
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word + PowerPoint
Output1-page Word update + 5-slide PPT outline
Review my Microsoft 365 activity from the past 5 business days (emails, meetings, and documents). Include Teams context only if available. Create: 1) A 1-page Word leadership update with: Key accomplishments, Risks & blockers, Decisions made, Open items, Recommended next steps. 2) A 5-slide PowerPoint outline: Status > Accomplishments > Risks > Decisions needed > Next steps. Write for a senior executive audience. Keep it concise and structured.

Prompt 3 — Weekly Executive Summary Dashboard (Safe HTML)

Best forA clean, browser-ready weekly report — no formatting dependencies
AppsOutlook + Calendar — HTML file output
OutputSelf-contained HTML report
Review my emails, calendar, and recent activity over the past 7 days. Summarize: key priorities, major meetings/outcomes (if identifiable), top collaborators, risks/follow-ups. Create a self-contained HTML report with: 1) Title: "Weekly Executive Summary" + date range 2) Key priorities section (bullets) 3) Activity table: Activity Type | Topic | Participants | Summary 4) Top 5 focus areas 5) Risks & follow-ups Design requirements: - Simple HTML with inline CSS only - Clean, modern formatting (black/white + one accent color) - No external libraries, scripts, fonts, or dependencies - Fully self-contained and easy to open in any browser If data is limited, infer where reasonable and clearly label assumptions.
✅ Pro tip — keep windows tight

Cowork is more accurate over 24 hours or 5 business days than over broader windows. If output feels diluted, narrow the time window and re-run with explicit names (your top brands, your top retailers, your top three direct reports).

⚠ Safety & control

Cowork may draft emails and files, but you stay in control. No emails are sent, no files are shared, and no calendar invites are created without your explicit approval. Treat all Cowork outputs as drafts until you've reviewed them.

🎯 Expected outcome

You have three Cowork prompts that, run on cadence (daily, weekly, weekly), become the operating rhythm of your week — a briefing every morning, a leadership update every Friday, an HTML dashboard you can share or save.

6

Meetings to Momentum Prep, recap, and follow through — Teams + Outlook + Cowork

▾
💡 Why this matters

Edgewell leaders spend the majority of their week in meetings — JBPs with retailers, brand reviews, S&OP, leadership team, board prep. The cost of a meeting isn't the hour; it's everything that doesn't happen afterwards because nobody wrote down what was decided. This lab fixes that.

Step 1 — Prep for the meeting (before)

Cowork prompt — Meeting Preparation & Follow-Through

Best forPreparing for an upcoming meeting and improving follow-up quality
AppsOutlook + Calendar + Word
OutputPrep brief + talking points + follow-up email draft
Prepare me for my upcoming meeting with [person/team, e.g., the Banana Boat brand team] on [date]. Summarize recent interactions (email + meetings), current topics, open threads, and unresolved questions. Provide: 1) 3 recommended talking points 2) 3 questions I should ask 3) Any risks/sensitivities to watch for Then draft a short follow-up email template I can send after the meeting. Keep it concise and practical.

Step 2 — Turn the meeting into decisions (during/after)

The "Meeting to Momentum" prompt:

Summarize all key decisions made in this meeting, including the rationale behind each decision. List every action item discussed, specifying the owner, deadline, and expected outcome. Highlight dependencies or cross-functional impacts. Draft a follow-up message for the leadership team that recaps these decisions and next steps, ready to send in Teams & Outlook.

Sharper follow-ups against the same recap:

What were the main concerns raised about the [Q3 forecast / promo plan / supply commitment]? Who committed to follow-up actions?
List all financial figures and metrics mentioned in this meeting in a table format.

Step 3 — Catch up on a channel where decisions live

What updates have been shared about the [Hawaiian Tropic 2026 launch / Schick promo readiness / Wet Ones channel plan] in the last 7 days? Summarize blockers, open items, or decisions made.

Step 4 — Triage your inbox into your day

From Copilot in Outlook — Prioritize Inbox:

Scan my Inbox folder and find emails from /[direct report or stakeholder]. Tell me what to handle first. Return: Focus Now (up to 5) — one line stating specifically what I need to do. If there is a clear due date, include it in the action statement. If not, try to infer when I should get the task done. Pick items only if there's a direct ask to me, a commitment I need to follow through on, or an ask I made that's still open. Be concise but sound like a human. No guesswork: base the next step on what's explicitly written. If specifics aren't given, don't invent them.

Then chain into action:

Which of those emails need my reply? For the ones that do, draft the reply for me.
Schedule a 30-minute meeting with [name] regarding the email you flagged as highest priority, and add a meeting agenda based on the email thread.
🎤 Facilitator note

If transcription isn't enabled on the participant's tenant, walk through this lab using a recently recorded meeting from your own tenant. The "meeting → recap → drafted follow-up in one motion" is the moment that sells the value.

⚠ Watch-out

Copilot in Teams requires transcription/recording to be enabled. If it's off for your tenant, the in-meeting prompts won't have anything to summarize. Confirm with IT before relying on this for live meetings.

🎯 Expected outcome

You walked into a meeting prepped (talking points, questions, risks), walked out with a clean recap of decisions + owners + dates, and sent a follow-up email in the same motion. No notes lost. No commitments missed.

7

Market & Competitive Edge Analyst + Researcher + Cowork market prompts — fuse internal signal with external reality

▾
💡 Why this matters

The CPG personal-care category moves fast: a competitor launches a sun-care SKU; a regulator changes a sunscreen-ingredient guideline; a retailer reshapes shelf in razors. Edgewell leaders who walk into reviews with the outside read tied to the inside read win the room. This lab gives you three engines for that — Analyst for the numbers, Researcher for the depth, Cowork for the cadence.

Engine 1 — Analyst Agent: numbers that move

Analyst is built for structured data — financials, SKU performance, channel splits. Attach an Excel file (or reference one with /) and let it think like a data scientist.

Prompt — Earnings & segment insight

Provide insights on [competitor]'s last 10-Ks and any updates in their latest quarterly filings. Focus only on segments relevant to our portfolio: shave/grooming, sun care, feminine care, skin care. Give me financial metrics and strategic initiatives related to those lines of business.

Prompt — Build a brand performance dashboard

Build a dashboard to visualize and analyze sales by month for the attached brand performance data. Focus on total revenue, gross margin, and unit velocity across our top 5 retailers. Call out any inflection points and recommend the 3 most important areas to investigate further.

Prompt — Scenario model

Using this financial data, model three scenarios for next fiscal year: conservative (2% net sales growth), base case (5% growth), and aggressive (9% growth). Show a comparison chart and summarize the key assumptions and financial impact for each scenario across our brand portfolio.

Engine 2 — Researcher Agent: depth on demand

Researcher does multi-step web + internal-file reasoning and cites every source. It can take 2–5 minutes; submit it early so results are ready by the time you return to this section.

Prompt — Industry benchmarks

Research current industry benchmarks for net sales growth, gross margin, and brand investment ratios in personal care — specifically shave/grooming, sun care, and feminine care. Compare these benchmarks against our company's performance using the attached financial summary. Provide recommendations on where we have room to improve and where we are performing above average.

Prompt — Competitive financial deep-dive

Analyze the financial performance of [Procter & Gamble's Grooming segment] and [Beiersdorf / Coty / Kenvue — pick relevant peer set] based on their latest publicly available earnings reports. Compare their net sales growth, segment margins, and brand investment trends over the last 4 quarters. Present findings in a structured comparison table and highlight areas where Edgewell could gain competitive advantage.

Prompt — Notebook with Researcher (multi-source)

Review the current filings of our main competitors in shave/grooming and sun care: compare their latest 10-K to their quarterly earnings and provide key takeaways. Focus on segment-level commentary, brand investment, channel dynamics (DTC vs. mass vs. drug), and any callouts on innovation or sustainability.

Innovation, Materials & Performance Tech Radar

Tips for better results

  • Keep time windows short — 24 hours or 5 business days is far sharper than "the past month."
  • Use 3–8 priorities at a time. Overly broad asks produce shallow output.
  • Name the brand, retailer, or initiative explicitly — vague references produce vague results.
  • "1 page max" or "assume I'll skim in 2 minutes" dramatically improves readability.
  • Review every draft before it goes out — especially emails, leadership updates, and anything stakeholder-facing.
  • If output feels thin, narrow the scope and re-run with tighter time windows or specific names.
  • For recurring outputs (daily brief, weekly update), save the prompt for quick reuse.

🛠 Troubleshooting

IssueLikely fix
Copilot says "I can't find that information" Make sure you're in Work mode (not Web mode) when searching organizational data. Files must be in OneDrive or SharePoint.
Draft email or summary lacks specific details Add more context: real names, actual numbers, real dates, exact brand/retailer references. Vague prompts produce vague output.
Copilot in Teams isn't available for a meeting Transcription or recording must be enabled. Check with IT if the feature is off in the tenant.
Analyst isn't analyzing the file Make sure the file is a structured Excel with clear column headers. Upload using + or reference from OneDrive using /.
Researcher is taking a long time Multi-step reasoning takes 2–5 minutes. Submit early and come back. This is expected, not broken.
Cowork output feels generic Narrow the time window, name 3–8 specific priorities or stakeholders, and add "1 page max". Re-run.
Cowork doesn't seem to "see" Teams activity Cowork's Teams context is opportunistic — if it isn't picking it up, name the channels or chats explicitly in your prompt.
Copilot icon isn't visible in Outlook or Teams Confirm your Microsoft 365 Copilot license is active. Try the web version if the desktop icon is missing.
Results seem inaccurate or incomplete Refine the prompt with sharper context. Break complex asks into smaller, focused prompts. Ask Copilot to cite its sources.
